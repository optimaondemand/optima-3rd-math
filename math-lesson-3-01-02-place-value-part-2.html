Essential Question
How many ways can you break apart a number?

Last lesson, you put each digit in one place. Today, you'll find out something surprising — the same number can be broken apart in more than one way.

4,326 isn't just 4 thousands. It can also be 43 hundreds. And the value never changes.

🪄

Every time you tap a trade button, the grouping changes — but the number at the top stays 4,326. That's the big idea.

Start: 4 thousands · 3 hundreds · 2 tens · 6 ones  = 4,326
✏️ After you trade a few times, write down one new way to break apart 4,326. Use words like thousands, hundreds, tens, ones.
📐 Key Concept — What You Just Saw

Every trade kept the number exactly the same.

That's because each place is worth 10 of the next smaller place:

1 thousand = 10 hundreds  ·  1 hundred = 10 tens  ·  1 ten = 10 ones

When you trade 1 piece for 10 smaller pieces, the value doesn't change — only the grouping. That's what regrouping means.

4,326 = 4 thousands + 3 hundreds + 2 tens + 6 ones  =  43 hundreds + 2 tens + 6 ones  =  432 tens + 6 ones

Worked Example: Back to the Bakery
Let's prove the cookie boxes from the warm-up.
Problem

Remember the bakery from the warm-up? It had 2,300 cookies packed two ways: 2 boxes of 1,000 + 3 boxes of 100 AND 23 boxes of 100. Let's prove both packs hold exactly 2,300 cookies.

Watch how each of the Math Crew thinks through this:

A
Atlas — concrete
"Build the standard way first: 2 thousand-discs and 3 hundred-discs on the mat. That's 2,300."
H
Hazel — pictorial
"Now trade. Each thousand-disc becomes 10 hundred-discs. Two trades give me 20 new hundreds, plus the 3 I started with — 23 hundreds total."

2,300 = 2 thousands + 3 hundreds = 23 hundreds

F
Felix — shortcut
"Quicker: 2,300 ÷ 100 = 23. Counts the hundreds without any trading."
C
Cora — checking
"Verify: 23 × 100 = 2,300. Matches the original count. ✓ Same cookies, different boxes."
💡

The trade and the division describe the same idea. Atlas does it with hands; Felix does it with arithmetic. Both arrive at 23 hundreds.

Essential Question
How do you find where a number lives on a number line?

A number line looks simple — just a line with some marks. But every number has its own home on it.

Today you'll learn the two things you need to find any number's home: the labels at each end, and the increment between the tick marks.

📐 Key Concept — What You Just Saw

Reading a number line has two steps:

Step 1. Read the labels at the ends.  ·  Step 2. Count the spaces between ticks, then divide the total distance by that number of spaces. That's the increment.

Once you know the increment, you can name any tick: just start at one of the labels and skip-count by the increment until you reach it.

4,000 to 5,000 with 5 spaces → 1,000 ÷ 5 = 200. So the ticks are 4,000 · 4,200 · 4,400 · 4,600 · 4,800 · 5,000.

📋

Worked Example: Where Does Mount Tyree Live?
Plot Mount Tyree (4,852 m) on a number line.
Problem

A number line stretches from 4,000 m to 5,000 m with 10 equal spaces. Where does Mount Tyree (4,852 m) belong?

Watch how each of the Math Crew finds the spot:

A
Atlas — concrete
"4,852 has 4 thousands, 8 hundreds, 5 tens, 2 ones. The 8 hundreds means we're past 4,800."
H
Hazel — pictorial
"Increment first: 1,000 ÷ 10 spaces = 100 per tick. Then skip-count: 4,000, 4,100, 4,200… 4,800, 4,900. Now I know which two ticks 4,852 sits between."
F
Felix — shortcut
"Skip the skip-counting. 4,852 is between 4,800 and 4,900. The 52 puts it just past halfway."
C
Cora — checking
"Check both distances. 4,852 − 4,800 = 52. 4,900 − 4,852 = 48. 48 < 52, so 4,852 is slightly closer to 4,900. The dot sits just past the midpoint. ✓"
💡

Plotting doesn't have to land exactly on a tick. 4,852 lives BETWEEN two ticks. You just place the point in the right neighborhood.

Essential Question
Does "fewer than" always mean subtract?

The words "more" and "fewer" don't always tell you the operation. Sometimes you ADD when you see "fewer." Sometimes you SUBTRACT when you see "more."

Today: trust the bar model, not just the words.

Why Option A works
Felix scored 17 FEWER in the 2nd game than in the 1st. That means the 1st game is the LONGER bar. The 2nd game's bar is 25. The 1st game's bar is 25 plus the difference of 17.
25 + 17 = 42  ·  Felix scored 42 in the first game.
✏️ How did the picture help you pick the operation?
📐 Key Concept

Four steps to solve a word problem:

1. Read it twice.   2. Find who's bigger.   3. Draw the bars.   4. Solve.

The bar shows which number is the LONG one and which is the SHORT one. Once you see that, the operation is obvious — even if the words tried to trick you.

Worked Example: Bakery Crepes
Tricky language: "fewer than."
Problem

A bakery sold 38 fewer sausage crepes than veggie crepes. It sold 55 sausage crepes. How many veggie crepes did it sell?

A
Atlas — read twice
"First read: bakery sold sausage and veggie crepes. Sausage is fewer. Second read: I know sausage = 55. I need to find veggie. Veggie is the LONGER bar."
H
Hazel — drawing it
"My bar: veggie on top (longer, unknown), sausage on bottom (55), and a 38-difference bracket. So veggie = sausage + 38 = 55 + 38."
F
Felix — the math
"From the bar, veggie = 55 + 38. Using break-apart: 55 + 30 = 85, then 85 + 8 = 93. Veggie crepes = 93."
C
Cora — checking the work
"Let's check by reversing. Veggie minus difference should equal sausage. 93 − 38 = 55. ✓ The story holds: 93 veggie crepes."

Veggie crepes: 93

💡

The story said "FEWER." But the unknown (veggie) was the BIGGER one. To find a bigger unknown, you ADD. The bar makes this obvious.

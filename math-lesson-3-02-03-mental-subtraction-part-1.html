Today's Mission
Subtract two numbers in your head — by counting up from the small one to the big one.
❓
Essential Question
How do you subtract in your head?

You could try to count down from the big number. But often it's much easier to count up from the small one.

Today: one simple strategy — and a new way to think about what subtraction actually means.

📐 Key Concept

Subtraction asks: "How far apart are these two numbers?"

Start at the small number. Count up. The total of your jumps is the difference.

For numbers that are CLOSE together (like 30 − 27 or 90 − 88), count up one by one. For numbers that are FARTHER apart, use friendly jumps — to the next ten first, then by tens.

📋

Worked Example: 81 − 67 (a bigger gap)
Use a friendly number as a stopping point.
Problem

Find 81 − 67. The gap is too big to count up one by one. We'll use a friendly number (70) as a stopping point.

A
Atlas — concrete
"I'm starting at 67 — that's the small number. The closest friendly number going up is 70. So my first jump is from 67 to 70. That's +3. Now I'm at 70."
H
Hazel — drawing it
"I'm at 70 and I want to reach 81. From 70 I can jump by tens. One ten gets me to 80. So my second jump is from 70 to 80. That's +10. Now I'm at 80."
F
Felix — finishing it
"I'm at 80 and I need to get to 81. That's just one more step. So my third jump is from 80 to 81. That's +1. My three jumps were +3, then +10, then +1. Add them: 3 + 10 + 1 = 14."
C
Cora — checking the work
"Let's check our answer. If 81 − 67 = 14, then 67 + 14 should give us 81. 67 + 10 = 77. 77 + 4 = 81. ✓ It works! The difference is 14."

81 − 67 = 14

💡

Three jumps: +3, +10, +1. Add them up to get the difference. The friendly number 70 was the "stepping stone" between 67 and 81.

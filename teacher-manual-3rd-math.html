How This Course Works
Lesson structure · typical week · major assessments

The Course at a Glance

OAO 3rd Grade Math follows Dimensions Math (Singapore Math) scope and sequence, aligned to Florida B.E.S.T. standards. The course runs 12 units across the school year. Every lesson is a self-contained HTML page hosted on GitHub Pages and embedded in Canvas as an iframe. Students work entirely independently — there is no synchronous class session. The teacher's role is to assign work in Canvas, grade paper-based assessments, and support students who need help.

A Typical Lesson

Each lesson page (e.g., Lesson 3.01.01 "Place Value, Part 1") opens in the Canvas iframe. The student sees:

  1. A progress bar at the top showing how far through the page they are.
  2. A header with the lesson title, unit, lesson number, Florida B.E.S.T. standard, and focus skill.
  3. An Explorer Strip ("Today's Mission") and Essential Question + Objectives card pair.
  4. A "You'll need" strip (typically: Math Journal, Pencil, Ruler or straight edge).
  5. Four tabs: Warm-Up → Learn → Practice → Show What You Know. (Explained in Section 2.)

The student moves through all four tabs in order. Tab checkmarks appear as they visit each tab. The Show What You Know tab has a turn-in checklist with confetti when all items are checked.

The Lesson Page Tour
Four tabs — what students do in each

Every lesson follows the same four-tab structure. The tab bar sticks at the top as the student scrolls. A checkmark (✓) appears on each tab after the student visits it. The tabs are designed to guide students through a complete Singapore Math lesson arc: readiness → concept → practice → application.

🔍 Warm-Up
📐 Learn
🧮 Practice
🌟 Show What You Know
← Students move left to right through these four tabs. Each tab's checkmark lights up when visited.
🔍 Tab 1 — Warm-Up

What's here: A timed or untimed fluency drill (addition, subtraction, or multiplication facts), a Readiness Check (a quick concrete/pictorial problem at the prior grade level), and a Think Prompt with a Notice/Wonder grid and a prediction box.

Purpose: The Readiness Check connects to what students already know (the bridge from 2nd grade or the prior lesson). The Notice/Wonder prompt activates curiosity before the concept is taught — students guess and record a prediction. This is the classical narration-before-instruction move: observe first, then learn.

Teacher note: None of this tab is collected or graded. Journal entries auto-save in the browser but are not submitted anywhere. If a student skips this tab, it shows up indirectly in their performance on the Practice tab — don't chase it as a missing assignment.

📐 Tab 2 — Learn

What's here: A concept video placeholder (placeholder text indicates video slots — videos are added as they are recorded), vocabulary flip cards (tap to reveal definition), a Key Concept box, interactive models (place-value mats, digit tiles, fraction strips, etc.), a Worked Example narrated by the Math Crew characters, a Socratic question, and a journal reflection prompt.

The CPA arc: Every Learn tab moves through Concrete (Touch It) → Pictorial (See It) → Abstract (Write It). Students build with on-screen manipulatives, read a chart representation, then write the standard/expanded form. This mirrors the Singapore Math CPA sequence.

Teacher note: The concept videos show as placeholders in lessons where they haven't been recorded yet. Students who encounter a video placeholder should still work through the rest of the tab — all the key concept content is also in the interactive and text elements on the page.

🧮 Tab 3 — Practice

What's here: Typically three interactive activities, each with its own Check button. When a student answers correctly, a green "pass" branch appears with positive feedback and the next-step nudge. When they answer incorrectly, an orange "retry" branch appears that re-teaches the specific concept and resets the problem for another attempt.

Teacher note: These are fully self-graded. Students can retry indefinitely. No attempt data is sent to Canvas — the practice is formative, not summative. A student who can pass all three activities in Practice is generally ready for the Concept Check on that lesson's skill.

🌟 Tab 4 — Show What You Know

What's here: A short set of written/drawn problems (usually 2–4), a "Connection Forward" note linking today's skill to the next lesson, a copy-your-work or PDF-download option, and a turn-in checklist. When all checklist items are ticked, confetti fires.

Teacher note: The Show What You Know tab is the bridge to the Independent Practice PDF. Students who use it well will copy their journal work and compare it to the IP problems. The tab itself is not collected — the Independent Practice PDF (submitted separately in Canvas) is the graded artifact.

💡

Biggest misconception for new teachers: The lesson page does not produce a submission. Students work on the HTML page, then download/print the Independent Practice PDF and submit that separately. If Canvas shows "no submission" for a lesson, check whether the IP PDF was submitted — not whether the iframe was opened.

The Math Crew
Four characters who model mathematical thinking

Every lesson's Learn tab and Worked Example sections feature four student characters called the Math Crew. They appear in thought bubbles (side-by-side panels) whenever a new problem is worked through. Their purpose is to model the range of valid strategies and show that mathematics has multiple entry points.

A — Atlas (Purple)
The Builder
Uses place-value mats, discs, and concrete tools. "I like to start with pieces I can see and touch." Represents the Concrete phase of CPA.
H — Hazel (Green)
The Diagrammer
Draws number lines, bar models, and charts. "I draw it out — a diagram helps me see." Represents the Pictorial phase of CPA.
F — Felix (Gold)
The Shortcutter
Looks for mental math strategies and patterns. "I find the quickest way — sometimes I do it in my head." Bridges Pictorial to Abstract.
C — Cora (Blue)
The Checker
Verifies answers and catches errors. "I make sure we're right — does the answer actually check out?" Represents mathematical precision and self-monitoring.
🎯

Why it matters for the teacher: When a student consistently uses only one strategy on their IP or assessments, you can nudge them toward another Crew member's approach. "Have you tried Hazel's way — can you draw a bar model for this?" The characters give you a shared vocabulary with students for talking about strategy flexibility without judgment.

Graded Items
Independent Practice · Concept Checks · Summative Assessments · Spotlight Tasks

Independent Practice PDFs (IP)

There is one IP PDF per lesson, named math-lesson-3-XX-XX-independent-practice.pdf. These are printed or uploaded student worksheets that parallel the lesson's Learn and Practice content. They are the primary formative assessment for each lesson.

Grading approach: IPs can be graded for accuracy (correct/incorrect per problem) or for completion (student showed work and attempted all problems). For most lessons, a spot-check of 3–4 key problems is sufficient. See the rubric in Section 6.

Concept Checks (CC)

One CC per unit, named math-3-XX-CC-concept-check.pdf. The CC is administered roughly at the unit midpoint (after the first 4 lessons of a longer unit, or after the full unit for shorter units). It is 4 pages, typically 8 problems, and covers the core concrete-to-abstract skills through models, standard form, expanded form, comparison, and explanation tasks.

Purpose: Catch gaps before the Summative Assessment. A student who misses 3+ problems on the CC should get targeted support before the SA.

Grading approach: Grade each problem right/wrong. For explanation questions (the final 1–2 items), use the rubric in Section 6. Total points and record in Canvas.

Summative Assessments (SA)

One SA per unit, named math-3-XX-SA-summative-assessment.pdf. The SA is 5 pages, 10 problems. It covers all unit skills and includes: show-your-work computation problems, a visual/drawing task, pattern/rule problems, word problems, an error-analysis item ("Find the Mistake"), and a multi-part reasoning item.

What makes the SA harder than the CC: The SA includes error analysis (identify and correct a wrong answer), multi-step reasoning, and "why are the answers different" explanation questions. These require full written responses, not just a number.

Grading approach: 1 point per problem for problems 1–8; 2 points for the final 2 reasoning/explanation problems. Maximum 12 points per SA. Use the answer guide in Section 6.

Spotlight Tasks (Weeks 17–32)

Spotlight Tasks are weekly VR activities embedded as iframes in Canvas. There are 32 Spotlight Task HTML files in the repository (two per week: a primary version and an alternate version labeled "-2"). The tasks run from Week 17 through Week 32 of the school year.

⚠️

Once the final VR content is added to these pages, update this section with the specific task for each week. The grading workflow below applies to the final version.

Once VR content is live — grading approach: Each Spotlight Task asks students to upload a file from their VR work (a screenshot, export, or recording). Review the uploaded file for evidence that the student completed the activity. Grade on a 3-point rubric: 3 = file submitted and shows clear engagement with the math task; 2 = file submitted but connection to math is unclear; 1 = file submitted but activity appears incomplete; 0 = no submission.

The two versions per week ("-2" variant) are alternates — students do one version, not both. [VERIFY which version is primary for each week once VR content is finalized.]

What Makes This Course Classical
Where classical pedagogy appears in the built lessons

Classical Moves That Are Actually in the Lessons

These are not abstract principles — they are features you can point to in every lesson HTML file.

Classical MoveWhere It AppearsWhat It Does
Notice/Wonder before instructionWarm-Up tab → Think About It sectionStudents observe a number, object, or situation and record what they notice and wonder before the concept is taught. This is narration before imitation — the classical sequence of observation preceding formal instruction.
CPA sequence (imitation before independence)Learn tab → Touch It / See It / Write It panelsEvery concept is taught Concrete first (build with discs), then Pictorial (read a chart), then Abstract (write the equation). Students imitate the concrete model before working independently in abstract form. This mirrors classical grammar-stage imitation.
Socratic questioningLearn tab → Socratic Question box (labeled "Stop and think")Each lesson has a genuine question that doesn't have an obvious answer — "Which digit has the greatest value, and how do you know?" Students write their thinking before the explanation is revealed. This is interrogative, not declarative, teaching.
Multiple perspectives on one problemMath Crew thought bubbles in Worked ExamplesThe same problem is solved four different ways (concrete, pictorial, shortcut, verification) in every worked example. Classical education prizes understanding a thing from multiple angles over mastering a single algorithm.
Virtue thread — mathematical characterMath Crew characters as recurring typesCora (the checker) models intellectual humility and precision. Felix models efficiency. Atlas models patience with concrete work. Hazel models the value of representing ideas visually. Together they model what a virtuous mathematical thinker looks like — careful, curious, creative, self-correcting.
Quote strips — classical openingBelow the header, above the info stripEach lesson opens with a short mathematical truth as a quote ("A digit tells more than its name. Its place tells its value."). This is the classical practice of beginning a lesson with a statement worth examining — the opening of the Great Conversation, even at 3rd grade.

How to Talk About This With Families

If families ask why the lessons start with questions rather than explanations, or why students are asked to guess before they're taught: this is deliberate. Classical math education holds that a student who has wrestled with a problem — even incorrectly — understands the answer more deeply when it arrives than a student who was told the rule first. The Notice/Wonder prompt and the Readiness Check are not filler. They are the pedagogical setup for genuine understanding.

How to Cultivate Mathematical Culture Asynchronously

In an async course with young students, culture comes through consistency and warmth, not live discussion. A few practices that work:

  • Reference the Math Crew by name in your Canvas announcements and feedback. "I saw some real Cora thinking in your work this week — you checked your rounding carefully."
  • When returning graded work, name the thinking that worked, not just the answer that was right. "You compared place by place starting from the thousands — that's exactly the strategy."
  • Occasionally share a quote from a student's explanation (anonymized and with permission) in your weekly announcement to model good mathematical writing for the cohort.
  • Hold the question open. When a student gets something wrong, respond with a question: "Which digit did you look at when you rounded? Let's start there."
